Chapter 2.1 – Budgeting, Savings and Investment

My Notes

Budgeting Basics!

Budgeting is the process of planning your pocket money or income and expenses to manage your money effectively. It starts by estimating your monthly income—this is the money you receive after taxes and deductions. Next, you list your expenses, which include fixed costs like rent and phone bills, and variable costs like groceries and entertainment. Tracking where your money goes helps you make informed decisions to save more and avoid overspending. Common budgeting methods include the 50/30/20 rule, where 50% of income goes to needs, 30% to wants, and 20% to savings and debt repayment.

Key Insight: Budgeting empowers you to control your finances, prioritize goals, and reduce financial stress.

Savings

What is Saving?

Saving means setting aside a part of your income regularly for future needs or emergencies. Building an emergency fund can protect you from unexpected expenses. Savings could be short-term for goals like vacations or long-term for buying a home or retirement. Automating your savings can help make regular saving easier and more consistent.

Why Save? Savings help you reach both short-term goals, like buying a new gadget or going on a trip, and long-term goals, like buying a bike, a car, or funding your college education. Building an emergency fund protects you from financial stress when life doesn’t go as planned. The earlier you start saving, the faster your money can grow, especially if it earns interest in a bank account.
Where to Save: For small, short-term savings, you might use a piggy bank or cash envelope at home. For larger amounts or long-term savings, it’s better to use a financial institution like a bank, because your money is safer and can earn interest, helping it grow over time.
How Savings Accounts Work: When you deposit money in a savings account, the bank pays you interest. This means the bank uses your money to lend to others, and as a reward, you receive a small extra amount, called interest. The amount you earn depends on the bank’s interest rate—some banks pay very little, while others pay more. Not only do you earn interest on your original deposit, but also on the interest you already earned—this is called compounding interest, and it helps your savings grow even faster.
How Much Should You Save? Experts recommend starting with an emergency fund, which is generally 3–6 months’ worth of living expenses. After building your emergency fund, set regular savings goals, such as saving 10% of your monthly income if possible. Automating your savings—such as setting up an automatic transfer to your savings account—can help you stay consistent.
Making Saving a Habit: Treat saving as a priority, not something you do only after all spending. Decide an amount or percentage to save every time you receive money, and stick to it. Remember: every rupee saved brings you closer to your financial goals and helps you feel prepared and confident about your future.

Investment

The Basics of Investing

Investment involves using your saved money to buy assets like stocks, bonds, or mutual funds, aiming to grow your wealth over time. Unlike savings, investments carry risks but offer the potential for higher returns. Understanding investment basics and risks helps you make informed choices that align with your financial goals and timeframe.

Real-Life Example: “Aarav’s Lemonade Stand”

Aarav loves making lemonade. During summer vacation, he decides to open a lemonade stand outside his house.

Aarav has ₹200 in his piggy bank. He uses ₹150 to buy lemons, sugar, and cups — this is his investment. He sells lemonade to his neighbors and earns ₹300 in total.

Now, let’s see what happened:

  • He spent ₹150 (his investment).
  • He earned ₹300 from selling lemonade.
  • So, he made a profit of ₹150 — that’s the return on his investment!

By investing his money wisely, Aarav made his savings grow.
